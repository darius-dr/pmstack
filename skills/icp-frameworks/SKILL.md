# ICP & copy frameworks

The frameworks pmstack subagents actually reach for. Not exhaustive — opinionated.

## Jobs-to-be-done (JTBD)

**Use for:** every ICP brief. Frames the audience around motivation, not demographics.

**Format:**
> When {situation}, I want to {motivation}, so I can {outcome}.

**Good example (real-estate B2C):**
> When I've found a property I might buy, I want to verify what could go wrong with it, so I can either negotiate the price down or walk away before I sign.

**Bad example (too vague):**
> When I'm buying property, I want information, so I can be informed.

**Probes** (what to ask in interviews):
- What triggered you to start looking?
- What did you do BEFORE you found us / a tool like this?
- What were you afraid of?
- What would have happened if you didn't find a solution?

## PAS — Problem · Agitate · Solution

**Use for:** hero sections, especially B2C. Names the pain before the offer.

**Structure:**
1. **Problem** (1 sentence): name the pain.
2. **Agitate** (1-2 sentences): make the cost of inaction concrete.
3. **Solution** (1 sentence): position the product as the path out.

**Example (real-estate hero, B2C buyers):**
> The seller won't tell you what's wrong with the property. (P)
> You find out about hidden easements, liens, or restrictions only after you've already wired €200,000+. (A)
> Acme pulls the facts from official sources into one clear report in ~5 minutes. You decide. (S)

**Where PAS fails:**
- B2B pro audiences (they don't want pain named for them).
- Products with low emotional stakes (don't agitate over a productivity app).

## BAB — Before · After · Bridge

**Use for:** value-prop sections, especially when the change is concrete. Multi-pillar layouts.

**Structure (per pillar):**
1. **Before:** the world as it is.
2. **After:** the world post-product.
3. **Bridge:** the specific mechanism that gets you there.

**Example (real-estate value-props):**
- **Before:** "The answers are scattered across ten different portals and registries."
- **After:** "All in one clear report — with a link to every source."
- **Bridge:** "Automated data pulls from the land registry, the cadastre, and mapping services."

**Where BAB shines:** when the product's value is the workflow change, not the feature list.

## AIDA — Attention · Interest · Desire · Action

**Use for:** pricing pages, last-chance CTAs.

**Structure:**
1. **Attention:** grab — a headline that stops the scroll.
2. **Interest:** the one differentiated reason to keep reading.
3. **Desire:** make the outcome vivid.
4. **Action:** a single, clear next step.

**Example (real-estate pricing):**
- **A:** *€49 for a report that saves €1,000+ at the negotiating table.*
- **I:** In ~5 minutes — data from 8 official sources, an AI summary, plain-language risk explanations.
- **D:** Buying a €200,000 property with no due diligence risks far more than a lawyer's fee. With Acme, you get the facts before you sign.
- **A:** *Check a property →*

**Where AIDA fails:**
- Cold-traffic hero sections (jumps to Desire too fast; PAS reads better).
- Long-consideration B2B (Action button isn't the next step; lead capture is).

## Value-prop canvas (compact)

**Use for:** locking the product-to-audience fit before writing copy.

**Sketch per audience:**

```
AUDIENCE: {segment}
┌───────────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┐
│ JOBS                          │ GAINS the product delivers    │
│ - {functional}                │ - {how the product helps}     │
│ - {emotional}                 │                               │
│ - {social}                    │                               │
├───────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
│ PAINS                         │ PAIN RELIEVERS                │
│ - {what hurts now}            │ - {what we take off the plate}│
│ - {risks / fears}             │                               │
└───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┘
```

If a pain has no reliever, the product isn't a fit for that audience. If a gain has no job behind it, you're selling a feature without a use case.

## "Test driving" your copy

After writing any section, run these:

1. **The 5-second test.** Show the section to a stranger for 5 seconds. Can they say what this is + who it's for? If no, rewrite.

2. **The "delete a sentence" test.** Remove a sentence at random. Does meaning change? If no, the sentence shouldn't be there.

3. **The "specificity test."** Replace every adjective with the most generic version of itself. Does the copy still differentiate? If yes, the original adjectives weren't doing work — find sharper ones.

4. **The "swap company" test.** Replace your product name with a competitor's. Does the copy still make sense? If yes, you're describing a category — find the differentiation.

5. **The "evidence test."** For every claim, can you point to evidence in the repo? If not, mark `[needs proof]` and stop.

## Tone calibration

- **B2C anxious buyer:** warm, plain, decisive. Avoid jargon. Acknowledge the stakes without amplifying them.
- **B2B pro broker:** time-efficient, factual, peer-to-peer. Pros despise being explained to.
- **B2B pro developer:** technical, data-forward, ROI-aware. Show the math.
- **Mixed audience hero:** lead with the dominant audience, surface the others via a segmented control or audience band.

---

## Banned moves (across all frameworks)

- Speaking in the third person about the audience ("Buyers love…"). Address them.
- Conflating the brand voice with the audience's voice. Customers don't say "leverage".
- Generic emotional appeals ("peace of mind", "confidence"). Be specific about what enables the feeling.
- Stacking adjectives ("simple, fast, reliable, modern"). Pick one and prove it.
- Negative competitor language by name. Position against the category, not a named rival.
